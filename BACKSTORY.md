# The Fading of Aurelune: A Chronicle of the Last Ember

Long ago, in an age so distant that even the world has forgotten its true name, there stood a radiant kingdom called **Aurelune**, a realm blessed by the **Ever Sun**, a holy light that never set. Its people knew no true night. Fields ripened year round, roads were safe, and the bells of the **Church of the First Dawn** rang over a land that believed itself chosen above all others.

At the center of this golden age sat **King Aldric the Fifth**, a just ruler loved by his people, and beside his throne stood the most powerful man in the kingdom, **Archbishop Lazaruz**, shepherd of the Church and keeper of the sacred rites.

But light breeds pride as easily as darkness breeds fear.

Lazaruz became obsessed with one question no prayer could silence. What lay beyond death, and why had the gods hidden it from men? At first, he called his studies holy. He claimed he sought wisdom to protect the kingdom from plague, war, and the grave itself. The king allowed it. The Church blessed it. Scholars praised it. Fools often call the first crack in the wall a window.

Beneath the cathedrals of Aurelune, in crypts older than the kingdom, Lazaruz uncovered forbidden rites left behind by a forgotten people who worshipped not the gods of light, but the restless dead. Through blood, incense, and stolen scripture, he reached beyond the veil and spoke to spirits that should never have answered.

They promised him truth.

They promised him power.

They promised that death itself could be chained.

What answered Lazaruz was not wisdom, but hunger.

The spirits seeped into the Church like rot into wet wood. Priests became mediums. Sacraments became bindings. Prayers became bargains. The holy oil on the altars was replaced with grave ash. The hymns of dawn were sung backward in the catacombs, and those who joined the rites returned changed, their eyes pale, their voices hollow, their faith twisted into fanatic devotion.

When King Aldric finally saw what Lazaruz had become, it was already too late.

On the night that should have been the kingdom’s holiest feast, Lazaruz performed his grand rite within the **Sanctum of the First Light**, using the king himself as the final vessel. The spell did not kill Aldric. It infested him. It lodged in his blood, burrowed into his soul, and bound his flesh to the will of the dead. Before the court, before the clergy, before the terrified people of Aurelune, the king died standing and rose again as something unholy.

He became the **Undead King**, the first and greatest servant of the darkness Lazaruz had invited into the world.

At that same moment, the Ever Sun vanished.

No eclipse came. No storm crossed the heavens. The light simply failed, as though the sky itself had turned its face away. Dawn never returned. The fields withered. Rivers blackened. Wolves grew bold. Graves opened from within. The kingdom of endless day became a land of eternal night.

Now Aurelune is remembered only by darker names. **The Night Crown. The Hollow Realm. The Kingdom Beneath No Dawn.**

Lazaruz still rules from the shattered cathedral city, not as a humble servant of the gods, but as the **Black Archbishop**, high priest of a dead court and master of a church that now worships the veil between life and death. The Undead King remains upon his throne, crowned in rusted gold, half monarch and half prison, for the curse inside him is both his torment and the chain that keeps the darkness anchored to the land.

The old noble houses have become vampire courts, grave knight orders, and feuding necromantic bloodlines. The common people survive in torchlit villages, mountain sanctuaries, and hidden monasteries where scraps of the old faith still endure. They whisper of relics from the Age of Light, seven sacred flames, the shattered crown of Aldric, and a prophecy that says night can be broken only when the king is laid to rest, the false church is burned out at its roots, and the sun is called back by mortal hands.

That is where the game begins.

Not in glory, but in the dying dark.

The players are not merely treasure hunters or wandering swords. They are living sparks in a dead kingdom. Perhaps they are descendants of fallen knights. Perhaps they are escaped acolytes of Lazaruz. Perhaps one of them bears a mark tied to the king’s curse. Whatever their origins, they are among the few left who can still choose what the world becomes.

In a land where dawn has been murdered, even a candle is an act of rebellion.
